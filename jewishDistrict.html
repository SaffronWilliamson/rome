History

The Jewish presence in Rome dates back over 2,000 years, making it one of the oldest Jewish communities in Europe. In 1555, Pope Paul IV ordered the creation of the Ghetto of Rome, forcing the Jewish population to live within a confined area with strict restrictions on their rights and professions. The ghetto was surrounded by walls and frequently subjected to floods from the nearby Tiber River. The ghetto was finally abolished in 1870, when Italy was unified, granting Jews full citizenship. Today, it stands as a testament to the endurance and cultural richness of the Jewish community in Rome.

Landmarks & Attractions

Great Synagogue of Rome

  • Built in 1904, this magnificent synagogue is one of the largest in Europe.
  • It houses the Jewish Museum of Rome, displaying centuries of Jewish history and artifacts.
  • Its distinctive square dome makes it a unique feature in the city's skyline.

Portico of Octavia

  • Originally built in the 2nd century BC, this ancient structure once housed a library and temples dedicated to Jupiter and Juno.
  • Over time, it became the heart of the Jewish Ghetto and now stands as a historic landmark.

Turtle Fountain (Fontana delle Tartarughe)

  • A beautiful Renaissance fountain in Piazza Mattei, dating back to the late 16th century.
  • The fountain’s charming bronze turtles were added in the 17th century, attributed to Bernini.

Via del Portico d’Ottavia

  • The main street of the Jewish Ghetto, lined with traditional kosher restaurants, bakeries, and historical sites.
  • A perfect place to experience Jewish-Roman culture and cuisine.

Sant’Angelo in Pescheria Church

  • A medieval church built over ancient ruins, with connections to the ghetto’s history.
  • The name "Pescheria" refers to the nearby ancient fish market.

Jewish-Roman Cuisine

  • Carciofi alla Giudia – Crispy deep-fried artichokes, a signature dish of the ghetto.
  • Filetti di Baccalà – Fried cod fillets, a popular local delicacy.
  • Torta di Ricotta e Visciole – A delicious ricotta and sour cherry tart, a traditional Jewish dessert.
  • Pizza Ebraica – A sweet fruit and nut-studded pastry, unique to the Jewish bakeries in Rome.

Legends & Curiosities

  • The Forced Sermons: During the ghetto era, Jews were required to listen to sermons aimed at converting them to Christianity in the Church of Sant’Angelo in Pescheria.
  • Turtle Fountain’s Secret: Legend has it that Duke Mattei had the fountain built overnight to impress his future father-in-law, proving his wealth and power.
  • The October 16, 1943 Tragedy: A dark moment in the ghetto’s history occurred during World War II, when more than 1,000 Jews were deported to Auschwitz by Nazi forces.

Visiting Tips

  • Best Time to Visit: Late morning or early evening to experience the neighborhood’s atmosphere and enjoy a meal at one of the traditional restaurants.
  • Jewish Museum of Rome: Fried cod fillets, a popular local delicacy.
  • Respect the Culture: The area remains a vibrant center of Jewish life, so visitors should be respectful of local customs, especially near religious sites.
  • Nearby Attractions: The Jewish Ghetto is close to the Tiber Island, Campo de’ Fiori, and Piazza Venezia, making it easy to explore other parts of central Rome.
