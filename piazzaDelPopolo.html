-Piazza del Popolo-

History

Piazza del Popolo was originally designed as the northern entrance to Rome, where travelers from the Via Flaminia would first set foot in the city. The square’s layout was significantly transformed in the 16th and 19th centuries, shaping it into the elegant space we see today. One of its most notable elements is the Flaminian Obelisk, an ancient Egyptian obelisk brought to Rome by Emperor Augustus in 10 BC. Originally erected in the Circus Maximus, it was later moved to the piazza by Pope Sixtus V in the late 16th century as part of his grand urban planning projects. The square’s name is often linked to the Church of Santa Maria del Popolo, a Renaissance masterpiece that houses works by Caravaggio and Raphael. Some theories suggest the name “Popolo” derives from the Latin word for “poplar trees,” which once grew in the area.

Architectural Marvels

The Twin Churches

  • The two Baroque churches, Santa Maria in Montesanto and Santa Maria dei Miracoli, stand symmetrically on either side of Via del Corso’s entrance.
  • Though they appear identical, small architectural differences set them apart.

The Flaminian Obelisk

  • Standing at 36 meters (118 feet), this obelisk is one of the tallest in Rome.
  • Originally from the Temple of Ra in Heliopolis, Egypt, it was transported to Rome by Emperor Augustus.

The Porta del Popolo

  • The grand entrance to the square, formerly known as the Flaminian Gate, was redesigned by Gian Lorenzo Bernini in 1655 to welcome Queen Christina of Sweden to Rome.
  • The inscription on the gate, commissioned by Pope Alexander VII, translates to: “Happy and Blessed Rome.”

The Fountains

  • Neptune Fountain: Features the Roman god of the sea flanked by two tritons.
  • Fountain of Rome Between the Tiber and the Aniene: Symbolizing the two main rivers supplying the city with water.
  • Lion Fountains: Surrounding the obelisk, these four fountains were designed by Giuseppe Valadier.

Fun Facts & Legends

  • The Ghost of Nero: According to legend, the notorious emperor Nero was buried near the site of the Church of Santa Maria del Popolo, and his ghost haunted the area until the church was built.
  • Dante’s Inferno: The castle was mentioned in Dante Alighieri’s Divine Comedy, reflecting its ominous reputation in medieval times.
  • A Stage for Celebrations: Piazza del Popolo has been a location for public executions, festivals, and military parades over the centuries.
  • Napoleon’s Influence: The present elliptical layout of the square was designed by Giuseppe Valadier in the early 19th century, partly inspired by French urban design.

Visiting Tips

Best Time to Visit

  • Sunset or early evening – The lighting enhances the beauty of the obelisk and surrounding architecture.
  • Spring and autumn – Ideal weather for exploring the piazza and nearby attractions.

Nearby Attractions

  • Villa Borghese – A short walk up the steps behind the piazza leads to Rome’s largest park and Pincio Terrace, offering breathtaking panoramic views.
  • Via del Corso – One of Rome’s most famous shopping streets, starting from the piazza.
  • Piazza di Spagna & the Spanish Steps – A 10-minute walk away, perfect for a combined visit.
