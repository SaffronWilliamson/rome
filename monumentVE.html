-Monument of Vittorio Emanuele II-

History

Commissioned in 1885 and inaugurated in 1911, the monument was designed by architect Giuseppe Sacconi to commemorate the unification of Italy. The construction took several decades and was completed in 1935. Built entirely of white Botticino marble, the Vittoriano was meant to symbolize Italy’s newfound unity and strength. Throughout the years, the monument has played a central role in national celebrations, hosting official ceremonies and military parades. It also serves as the location of the Tomb of the Unknown Soldier, established in 1921 to honor the fallen soldiers of World War I.

Architectural Highlights

The Colossal Equestrian Statue of Vittorio Emanuele II

  • At the center of the monument stands an enormous bronze equestrian statue of King Vittorio Emanuele II.
  • The statue is 12 meters long and 10 meters high, symbolising the king’s leadership in unifying Italy.
  • A fascinating fact: the horse’s stomach is large enough to fit a small table and was reportedly used for a celebratory meal during construction!

The Grand Staircase & Corinthian Columns

  • The front of the monument features a wide, majestic staircase leading to towering Corinthian columns, reminiscent of classical Roman architecture.

The Altar of the Fatherland & Tomb of the Unknown Soldier

  • At the heart of the Vittoriano lies the Altar of the Fatherland, adorned with an eternal flame and guarded by soldiers.
  • Beneath it rests the Tomb of the Unknown Soldier, a tribute to all Italian soldiers who perished in war without recognition.

The Panoramic Terraces

  • Visitors can take an elevator to the top for a stunning 360-degree view of Rome, with clear sights of landmarks like the Colosseum, Roman Forum, and St. Peter’s Basilica.

Museo del Risorgimento

  • Inside the monument, this museum showcases Italy’s journey to unification through documents, artifacts, and historical exhibits.

Legends & Curiosities

  • The "Typewriter" and "Wedding Cake" Nicknames: Due to its massive white marble structure, locals humorously compare the monument to a typewriter or a tiered wedding cake.
  • Mussolini’s Speeches: During Fascist rule, dictator Benito Mussolini often delivered speeches from the Vittoriano’s balcony.
  • Controversial Construction: Many historic buildings, including medieval churches, were demolished to make way for the monument, leading to criticism over its impact on Rome’s ancient cityscape.

Visiting Tips

  • Best Time to Visit: Visit early in the morning or late afternoon for cooler temperatures and fewer crowds.
  • Panoramic Elevator: Don’t miss the elevator to the terrace for some of the best views of Rome.
  • Free Entry: Access to the main monument and the Tomb of the Unknown Soldier is free, but a ticket is required for the museum and panoramic terrace.
  • Nearby Attractions: The Roman Forum, Capitoline Hill, and Piazza Venezia are all within walking distance.
