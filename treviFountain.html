-Trevi Fountain-

History

The Trevi Fountain (Fontana di Trevi) is Rome’s most famous fountain, an iconic masterpiece of Baroque architecture and a symbol of romance and legend. Its origins date back to 19 BC, when Emperor Augustus’ general, Marcus Agrippa, commissioned the Aqua Virgo aqueduct, which brought fresh water to the city. The current fountain was designed in 1732 by Nicola Salvi and completed in 1762 by Giuseppe Pannini, under the patronage of Pope Clement XII. The fountain stands at the end of the Aqua Virgo aqueduct, which still supplies it with fresh water today. The name “Trevi” comes from “Tre Vie” (Three Roads), referring to the three streets that converge at the fountain’s location.

Architectural Marvels

The Grand Design

  • The Trevi Fountain stands at 26.3 meters (86 feet) high and 49.15 meters (161 feet) wide, making it the largest Baroque fountain in Rome.
  • It is set against the Palazzo Poli, which enhances its dramatic, theatrical effect.

Main Sculptural Elements

  • Oceanus, the God of Water – The central figure, riding a shell-shaped chariot pulled by two seahorses.
  • Two Tritons – One leads a calm seahorse, the other a wild seahorse, symbolizing the changing moods of the sea.
  • Abundance & Health – The two side statues represent Abundance (left), spilling water from a jar, and Salubrity (right), holding a cup from which a snake drinks.
  • Bas-Reliefs – Above the statues, bas-reliefs depict Agrippa ordering the construction of the aqueduct and a young girl (Virgo) showing soldiers the water source.

Fun Facts & Legends

  • Throwing a Coin: According to tradition, tossing a coin over your left shoulder into the fountain ensures a return to Rome. Throwing two coins means you’ll find love, and three means marriage! Each year, around €1.5 million in coins are collected and donated to charity.
  • Movie Fame: The fountain gained worldwide fame thanks to its appearance in Federico Fellini’s “La Dolce Vita” (1960), in which Anita Ekberg wades through its waters.
  • Papal Influence: Pope Urban VIII originally wanted the fountain to be much grander, but his vision was never realised due to funding issues.

Visiting Tips

Best Time to Visit

  • Early morning or late at night to avoid heavy crowds.
  • Evening visits are magical, as the fountain is beautifully illuminated.

    • Nearby Attractions

  • Piazza di Spagna & Spanish Steps – A short walk away, perfect for exploring more Baroque beauty.
  • Pantheon – Just a 10-minute walk, offering another glimpse into Rome’s ancient splendor.
  • Via del Corso – A famous shopping street nearby, ideal for a stroll.
