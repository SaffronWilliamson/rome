-Piazza Navona-

History

Piazza Navona stands on the site of the ancient Stadium of Domitian, built in the 1st century AD for athletic competitions. The stadium could hold up to 30,000 spectators and was used for running events and other sports. Over time, the area was repurposed into a public square, maintaining the elongated shape of the original stadium. During the 17th century, under the patronage of Pope Innocent X, the square underwent a grand transformation into a Baroque masterpiece. Many of the stunning architectural and artistic features seen today date back to this period.

Main Attractions

Fountain of the Four Rivers (Fontana dei Quattro Fiumi)

  • Designed by Gian Lorenzo Bernini in 1651, this magnificent fountain is the centerpiece of the piazza.
  • It represents four major rivers from different continents: the Nile (Africa), Danube (Europe), Ganges (Asia), and Río de la Plata (Americas).
  • The fountain is topped with an ancient Egyptian obelisk, adding to its grandeur.

Church of Sant'Agnese in Agone

  • A stunning Baroque church designed by Francesco Borromini, located right in front of Bernini’s fountain.
  • It is dedicated to Saint Agnes, who, according to legend, was martyred on this very site.
  • The church’s striking facade and elegant dome make it a prominent feature of the square.

Fontana del Moro & Fontana del Nettuno

  • At the southern end, the Fontana del Moro features a Moor wrestling a sea creature, sculpted by Giacomo della Porta and later enhanced by Bernini.
  • At the northern end, the Fontana del Nettuno depicts Neptune battling a sea monster, a later addition to the piazza.

Fun Facts & Legends

  • A famous urban legend suggests that Bernini designed the figures on the Fountain of the Four Rivers in a way that they appear to be reacting negatively to Borromini’s nearby church. However, this is purely a myth, as the fountain was completed before the church.
  • In past centuries, the piazza was sometimes intentionally flooded during summer to provide a cooling effect for the city’s elite.
  • Today, Piazza Navona is a hub for street artists, performers, and bustling cafés, making it a perfect spot for people-watching and soaking in the Roman atmosphere.

Visiting Tips

  • Best Time to Visit: Early morning for a peaceful experience, or evening to enjoy the lively ambiance and illuminated fountains.
  • Nearby Attractions: The Pantheon, Campo de' Fiori, and Trevi Fountain are all within walking distance.
  • Dining: Try a classic Roman meal at one of the piazza’s restaurants, though for a more authentic experience, consider exploring nearby side streets for hidden gems.
  • Events: Piazza Navona hosts a famous Christmas market in December, with festive decorations, stalls, and entertainment.
