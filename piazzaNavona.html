-Piazza Navona-

History

Piazza Navona is one of Rome’s most famous and picturesque squares, known for its Baroque architecture, fountains, and vibrant atmosphere. The site originally housed Stadium of Domitian, built in 86 AD to host athletic competitions. The stadium could accommodate up to 30,000 spectators, and its distinctive oval shape remains preserved in today’s square. In the 15th century, the stadium ruins were transformed into a public square, becoming a center for markets, festivals, and public gatherings. By the 17th century, under the patronage of Pope Innocent X, Piazza Navona was redesigned into the stunning Baroque masterpiece that we see today.

Architectural Marvels

The Fountains

  • Fountain of the Four Rivers (Fontana dei Quattro Fiumi) – Designed by Gian Lorenzo Bernini in 1651, this is the centerpiece of the piazza. It represents the four great rivers of the world:
    • - Nile (Africa) – A veiled figure, symbolizing the river’s unknown source.
    • - Danube (Europe) – The most “honored” river, as it touches Catholic lands.
    • - Ganges (Asia) – Holding an oar, representing navigability.
    • - Rio de la Plata (Americas) – Sitting on coins, symbolizing the riches of the New World.
    • - The fountain is topped by a towering Egyptian obelisk, adding to its dramatic presence.
  • Fountain of Neptune (Fontana del Nettuno) – Created in 1574, but its statues of Neptune battling sea creatures were added in the 19th century.
  • Fountain of the Moor (Fontana del Moro) – Originally designed in 1575 by Giacomo della Porta, with later additions by Bernini, featuring a Moor wrestling a dolphin.

Church of Sant'Agnese in Agone

  • Overlooking the piazza, this stunning Baroque church was designed by Francesco Borromini, a rival of Bernini. It was built on the legendary spot where Saint Agnes was martyred in 304 AD. The church’s convex façade and majestic dome add to the grandeur of Piazza Navona.

Fontana del Moro & Fontana del Nettuno

  • At the southern end, the Fontana del Moro features a Moor wrestling a sea creature, sculpted by Giacomo della Porta and later enhanced by Bernini.
  • At the northern end, the Fontana del Nettuno depicts Neptune battling a sea monster, a later addition to the piazza.

Palazzo Pamphilj

  • This grand 17th-century palace was once the residence of Pope Innocent X’s family. Today, it serves as the Brazilian Embassy, but its opulent frescoed interiors by Pietro da Cortona are worth admiring.

Fun Facts & Legends

  • A Rivalry in Stone? It’s said that Bernini’s Fountain of the Four Rivers features a figure shielding its face from Borromini’s Church of Sant'Agnese, symbolizing their artistic rivalry. However, this is likely just a myth, as the fountain was completed before the church’s façade.
  • Flooded Piazza – Until the 19th century, Piazza Navona was occasionally flooded in the summer for naval games and cooling off in the heat.
  • Christmas Market – Every December, the piazza transforms into a festive Christmas market, filled with stalls selling treats, decorations, and toys.

Visiting Tips

Best Time to Visit

  • Evening: The fountains are beautifully lit at night, and street performers add to the lively atmosphere.
  • Early Morning: A quieter time to appreciate the art and architecture without the crowds.

Nearby Attractions

  • Pantheon – A short walk away, one of Rome’s most iconic ancient landmarks.
  • Campo de' Fiori – A bustling market square, perfect for exploring Roman street life.
  • Trevi Fountain – A must-visit masterpiece, just a 15-minute walk from Piazza Navona.
