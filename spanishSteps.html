-Spanish Steps-

History

The Spanish Steps (Scalinata di Trinità dei Monti) are one of Rome’s most famous landmarks, known for their elegant design and lively atmosphere. The staircase of 135 steps was built between 1723 and 1725, designed by Francesco de Sanctis and financed by Étienne Gueffier, a French diplomat. The steps were constructed to connect Piazza di Spagna (below) with the Trinità dei Monti Church (above). Despite their name, the Spanish Steps were funded by the French and are located near the Spanish Embassy to the Holy See, which influenced their name. Over the centuries, the Spanish Steps became a meeting place for artists, poets, and travelers, and today, they remain one of the most visited and photographed sites in Rome.

Architectural Marvels

The Grand Staircase

  • The 135-step staircase is designed in a butterfly-like formation, with wide terraces allowing visitors to pause and enjoy the view.
  • They serve as a dramatic entrance to the Trinità dei Monti Church, adding to their architectural elegance.

Trinità dei Monti Church

  • Located at the top of the steps, this beautiful Renaissance-style church was completed in the late 16th century.
  • It offers breathtaking views over Rome and is home to important works of art, including frescoes by Daniele da Volterra, a pupil of Michelangelo.

Fontana della Barcaccia

  • At the foot of the steps in Piazza di Spagna sits the Fountain of the Ugly Boat, designed by Pietro Bernini (father of the famous Gian Lorenzo Bernini) in the early 1600s.
  • The fountain is shaped like a half-sunken boat, inspired by a legend that a small boat was carried to this spot by the Tiber River during a flood.

Fun Facts & Legends

  • A Meeting Place for Artists: Throughout history, the Spanish Steps have attracted painters, writers, and photographers, drawn by the staircase’s charm and energy.
  • Keats and Shelley House: The famous English poet John Keats lived in a house next to the steps, now a museum dedicated to Keats and Percy Bysshe Shelley.
  • Springtime Blooms: Every spring, the steps are adorned with vibrant azalea flowers, enhancing their beauty and drawing even more visitors.
  • No Sitting Rule – In 2019, Rome banned sitting on the Spanish Steps to protect them from damage, with fines for violators.

Visiting Tips

Best Time to Visit

  • Early morning for a peaceful experience.
  • Evening visits are magical when the steps are illuminated.
  • Spring (April–May) is especially beautiful when azaleas bloom.

Nearby Attractions

  • Via Condotti – A famous street for luxury shopping, just in front of the steps.
  • Piazza del Popolo – A short walk away, home to obelisks and historic churches.
  • Trevi Fountain – Just a 10-minute walk, perfect for combining visits.
